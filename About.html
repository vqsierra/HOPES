Helping Our People End Suicide Study

The Helping Our People End Suicide (HOPES) Study aims to create a culture-based suicide screener for use at the Sacramento Native American Health Center (SNAHC). The study hopes to prevent suicide among Native American youth in the Sacramento community by promoting culturally relevant care. The newly developed screener will help to connect young people to the new youth services at SNAHC. 

The HOPES Study is a community-based participatory research project. It will center the youth voice and family perspective throughout the development of the new suicide screener. Youth, community and family members, as well as SNAHC staff are invited to participate. Personal experiences and community knowledge are essential in gaining a comprehensive understanding of how to best recognize and prevent suicide. 

Frequently Asked Questions

Who can participate?

Youth, community and family members, as well as SNAHC staff are invited to participate in the HOPES Study.

  • Youth Participant Eligibility:
    • Self-identify as Native American or Indigenous
    • Aged 13 - 24 years old; Minors aged 13-17 years old, consent of a parent or legal guardian is required
    • Primary residence in Sacramento County
  • Community Member Participant Eligibility:
    • Self-identify as Native American or Indigenous
    • Over the age of 18 years old
    • Primary residence in Sacramento County
  • SNAHC Staff Member Participant Eligibility:
    • Current employee at SNAHC
    • Over the age of 18 years old
    • Primary residence in Sacramento County
Will I get paid to participate?

All participants will be paid for their time and expertise. Participants will be compensated with a Target gift card. The amount of compensation depends on your level of involvement in the study. 

Youth will receive a $25.00 gift card for the completion of one survey and a one-on-one interview or a focus group. 

Community Members who complete two surveys will receive a $10.00 gift card. Community Members who complete the two surveys and a one-on-one interview or focus group will receive a $30.00 gift card.

SNAHC Staff Members who complete two surveys will receive a $10.00 gift card. SNAHC Staff Members who complete the two surveys and a one-on-one interview or focus group will receive a $30.00 gift card.

What kind of information will you collect?

The HOPES Study is a mixed-methods study that will use both qualitative and quantitative methods. Qualitative data involves peoples’ stories and personal experiences. Qualitative data includes numbers and statistics. 

Qualitative data will be gathered using one-on-one interviews. Focus groups will also be used. Quantitative data will be gathered using surveys and questionnaires. Participants will be asked about their opinions on current suicide screening protocols. Participants will also be asked about their ideas on how to improve culturally responsive care. Questions regarding about suicide stigma will also be asked.

How do I participate?

Please visit our Participate webpage for important links.

The HOPES Study will be conducted virtually using the Zoom and Qualtrics platforms. On the Participate   webpage, you will find a link to an external website to begin the onboarding process. First, you will complete a short questionnaire to make sure you are eligible to participate in the study. Next, you will complete the consent form process. For youth participants aged 13-17 years old, consent of a parent or legal guardian is required. 

After completing the consent form process, you will then be asked to complete one or two surveys. Research study staff may contact you directly to schedule a preferred time to for an interview or focus group.

Why is preventing suicide important?

Suicide remains an urgent mental health disparity for Native American young people. According to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, suicide is the leading cause of non-accidental death for youth ages 10 through 24. Compared to other ethnic-racial groups, suicide impacts Native American communities at the highest rate. Several studies also point out that suicide is more common in urban Native American communities. These statistics remain true in Sacramento County which is home to one of the many large, urban communities of Native American people in California. The HOPES Study looks to end suicide by identifying early warning signs and connecting youth in need of extra support to the necessary clinical and cultural services.

Collaborations & Researchers

Sacramento Native American Health Center, Inc.

SNAHC is a non-profit 501(c)(3) Federally Qualified Health Center committed to continue and share the legacy of a healthy American Indian / Alaska Native community based on cultural values delivered through a traditional, innovative and accessible patient-centered health home. SNAHC offers primary care, oral health, behavioral health, specialty, and supportive services in midtown Sacramento. We are open to all, and all are welcome. For more information on SNAHC, visit www.snahc.org or call 916-341-0575 for an appointment.

Valentín Quiroz Sierra

Val is a Ph.D. student at the UC Berkeley School of Social Welfare. Val’s clinical and research agendas focus on eliminating mental health disparities, particularly suicide and depression, for urban Native American young people through culturally grounded practices and interventions. Val holds a M.S.W. from Berkeley Social Welfare and a B.A. in Native American Studies, with highest honors, from UC Davis. Val is an Associate Clinical Social Worker and an active member of the California Yaqui (Yoeme) Indian community.
